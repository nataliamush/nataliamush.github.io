My Background

I graduated with High Honors from the University of California, Berkeley with a B.S. in Molecular Environmental Biology and a minor in Ecosystems Management and Forestry.

Education, Logistics, and Management

My most formative work experience has been as an educator - I was involved with UC Berkeley’s flagship introductory data science course, Data 8, for over three years. Starting as a TA, I learned to communicate complex ideas in an approachable, concise way, and was dedicated to showing students that data science is not the realm solely of computer science lovers, but a field that provides tools for anyone, in any field. Accessibility of the material was my #1 goal!

Eventually, I became a head TA, managing logistics for a course that had by then grown to 1500 students and 90+ staff members. This is when I realized I find operations/systems work invigorating! I loved writing scripts to automate processes that had become overwhelming for a course of that size, and enjoyed being a cog in the gargantuan organization that the course had become. Exam days, with all the logistics that go into getting 1500 students into their assigned locations, exams passed out, policies announced, proctors stationed, and later exams counted, scanned, and accounted for, were exciting (any Data 8 staff members reading this - I know, I’m a huge nerd). I’d love to keep working in operations, event planning, or project management!

Finally, I became one of two primary lecturers for the course the summer after graduating from Cal. Lecturing to a class of 300 students every other day was an incredible public speaking bootcamp, but I think I learned the most from co-leading a staff of 24 people. My staff was incredible - I would not have survived the intensity of that course without them - and it was amazing to see how delegation, project timelines, and regular check-ins made the course run smoothly. Massive thanks to my incredible colleagues!

Research and Data

Outside of teaching Data 8, I’ve spent a lot of time thinking about and participating in ecology research. I started in a community ecology lab at Cal, helping a graduate student (Lindsey Hendricks-Franco, an amazing mentor) with soil analysis of post-fire chaparral soils. Many of the skills I learned through that experience came in very handy for my work at the NOAA Beaufort laboratory under Dr. Jenny Davis and Dr. Carolyn Currin, where I studied belowground impacts of thin-layer sediment application, a coastal management technique, on North Carolina salt marshes. At Beaufort, I learned nutrient and soil analysis skills such as preparing samples for UV-Vis spectroscopy, determining soil characteristics such as moisture and texture, and preparing vegetation and soil samples for elemental analysis. The most rewarding part of the process was then writing a senior thesis about the work and presenting a lightning talk at the 2018 American Geophysical Union conference - once again, an exercise in communicating complex ideas clearly.

Working in research and taking statistics, probability, computer science, and data science classes has given me familiarity with data management, processing, and analysis. My primary languages are Python and R. I’d love to increase my GIS skills - I’m familiar with the basics of analysis and data management in ArcGIS, but am looking to better understand geostatistics.

Most importantly, studying ecology has given me the chance to work in some beautiful places - the best summer of my life was spent all over the state of California on UC Natural Reserves, learning to notice ecological patterns and formulate hypotheses. I believe that outdoor experiences can shape an ethos of reciprocity with land, and that this ethos is key to a non-exploitative culture of resource use.
